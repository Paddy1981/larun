ECSS Knowledge Hub

Complete reference for the European Cooperation for Space Standardization — the unified standards framework for all European space activities. Established 1993, managing 139 active standards across 7 branches.

Project planning, configuration management, cost & schedule, risk management. 6 active standards governing how space projects are organized and controlled.

Core Principle

ECSS requirements define what needs to be fulfilled, not how — leaving implementation flexibility to suppliers while ensuring consistent quality across European space programmes.

ECSS Organization Structure

Five Entities

Steering Board — Elaborates objectives, policy, strategies (one rep per member body; meets twice yearly)
Technical Authority (TA) — Implements Steering Board decisions; meets 4 times/year
Executive Secretariat — Administrative support, promotion, communication management
Network of Experts — Technical support to TA and secretariat
Working Groups — Initiated as needed for defining/updating specific standards

Document Architecture

How ECSS standards are structured, named, and organized into branches, disciplines, and document types.

Naming Convention

Document Identifier Format

ECSS-<Branch>-<Type>-<Number><Version> <Revision>

Example: ECSS-E-ST-10-02C Rev.1 = Engineering branch, Standard, System Engineering — Verification, Version C, Revision 1

FieldValuesDescription
BranchS, M, E, Q, UStandardization branch (also P, D for internal)
Document TypeST, HB, TM, AS, AHStandard / Handbook / Tech Memo / Adopted Std / Adopted HB
NumberXX or XX-XXDiscipline (2 digits) + optional sub-discipline (2 digits)
VersionA, B, C, ...Release version (most are at "C" currently)
RevisionRev.1, Rev.2, ...Updated release within same version

Document Types

TypeCodeNaturePurpose

Non-Tailorable Requirements

Space debris mitigation (ECSS-U-AS-10C / ISO 24113) and safety requirements concerning protection of human life are explicitly NOT subject to tailoring. They must always be fully applied regardless of mission complexity.

Tailoring Process

ECSS standards are intentionally generic. Tailoring adapts them to specific project needs through a formal 7-step process.

Governing Documents

ECSS-S-ST-00C Rev.1 — System description, implementation, general requirements
ECSS-S-ST-00-02C — ECSS System Tailoring (dedicated tailoring standard)

Key Tailoring Instruments

EARM (ECSS Applicability Requirements Matrix): Identifies which ECSS standards and individual requirements apply to a specific project. Included as part of the Project Requirements Document (PRD).


Standards Tailoring Baseline: Selection of applicable standards for the project.


Requirements Tailoring Baseline: Selection of applicable requirements within those standards.


Compliance Matrix: Supplier demonstrates compliance with tailored requirements.

Tailoring Drivers

  • Security requirements and classification
  • Dependability and safety targets
  • Development and programmatic constraints
  • Product quality objectives
  • Business needs, budget, and schedule
  • Heritage and reuse considerations
  • Mission criticality and risk appetite

Typical Tailoring Scale

Mission ComplexityApprox. RequirementsExample
Full complexity (flagship)25,000+Copernicus Sentinels, JUICE, ExoMars
Medium complexity~15,000Telecom satellites, Earth observation
Reduced complexity~10,000Technology demonstrators
Simple / IOD< 7,500In-Orbit Demonstration, CubeSat+

Customer–Supplier Model

The fundamental framework through which ECSS standards are applied in space programmes.

Core Principles

  • Every project actor is a customer, a supplier, or both
  • Standards become binding only through a business agreement or contract
  • The top-level customer's PRD forms the basis for all lower-level PRDs
  • Each PRD includes an EARM (ECSS Applicability Requirements Matrix)
  • Suppliers demonstrate compliance through a compliance matrix
  • The party imposing the standard is responsible for monitoring correct application
  • ECSS does not provide any certification or accreditation process
  • Reviews cascade top-down through the supplier chain

Typical Hierarchy

Project Initiator (e.g., ESA) → Top-Level Customer / Prime Contractor → 1st Level Suppliers (Subsystem Providers) → 2nd Level Suppliers (Equipment Providers) → nth Level Suppliers (Component Providers)

The customer always retains the right to attend any lower-level review, including those below the level of its direct suppliers.

Safety & Dependability (Q-30 / Q-40)

The core RAMS (Reliability, Availability, Maintainability, Safety) disciplines within product assurance.

FMEA/FMECA (Q-ST-30-02C)

Failure Modes, Effects (and Criticality) Analysis — a systematic bottom-up technique applied at all project levels:

Severity CategoryDescriptionExamples
1 — CatastrophicLoss of life or systemLoss of manned flight system, severe environmental effects
2 — CriticalMajor damage or lossLoss of mission, major environmental effects
3 — MajorSignificant degradationMission degradation, short-term environmental effects
4 — MinorMinor effectsAny other effects not covered above

Hazard Analysis (Q-ST-40-02C)

Comprises identification, classification, and reduction of hazards. Interfaces with dependability analysis (FMECA) and project risk management (M-ST-80). Safety risk assessment contributes to overall project risk management.

Fault Tree Analysis (Q-ST-40-12C)

Adoption of IEC 61025 — top-down deductive analysis method. Results can be integrated with system-level FTA. Software FTA (SFTA) uses same technique steps and formats as hardware FTA.

EN 16601-xx = M-branch (Management)
EN 16602-xx = Q-branch (Product Assurance)
EN 16603-xx = E-branch (Engineering)
